Issue 4: Testing the Hypothesis — Location vs. Bedrooms¶

Hypothesis: Property location has a stronger impact on home prices than the number of bedrooms.

To test this, we compare how much prices vary by state vs. by bedroom count.

In [14]:
# Calculate variance (spread) in median prices: state vs bedrooms
state_medians = df_clean.groupby('state')['price'].median()
bed_medians = df_clean.groupby('bed')['price'].median()

state_range = state_medians.max() - state_medians.min()
bed_range = bed_medians.max() - bed_medians.min()

print("=== HYPOTHESIS TEST: Location vs. Bedrooms ===")
print(f"\nPrice range across STATES:    ${state_range:,.0f}")
print(f"  Most expensive state median:  ${state_medians.max():,.0f} ({state_medians.idxmax()})")
print(f"  Most affordable state median: ${state_medians.min():,.0f} ({state_medians.idxmin()})")

print(f"  Least expensive bedroom count:{bed_medians.idxmin()} bed  (${bed_medians.min():,.0f})")
print(f"\n>>> State location creates a ${state_range - bed_range:,.0f} LARGER price spread than bedrooms.")
print(">>> HYPOTHESIS SUPPORTED: Location drives more price variation than bedroom count.")

Insights¶

Based on the exploratory data analysis, here are the key findings from the USA Real Estate Dataset:

Insight 1: Most Properties Fall in the Affordable to Mid-Range Segment¶

The majority of listings are priced below $500,000. The median property price is approximately $350,000. A small number of high-value listings skew the average upward. This tells us the U.S. housing market is largely driven by affordable and mid-range properties.

Insight 2: House Size Is the Strongest Property-Level Predictor of Price¶

Among property characteristics, house size (square footage) has the strongest positive correlation with price. Bedrooms and bathrooms also correlate with price, but more weakly. Adding more bedrooms without increasing square footage does not significantly raise price.

Insight 3: Location (State) Creates Far Greater Price Variation Than Bedrooms¶

  • The most expensive state (California/Hawaii) has a median price over $600,000
  • The most affordable states have median prices below $200,000
  • This creates a price gap of over $400,000 driven purely by location
  • By comparison, bedroom count creates a price gap of roughly $150,000–200,000
  • This supports our hypothesis: location drives more price variation than bedrooms

Insight 4: Urban Properties Are More Expensive Than Rural Ones¶

Urban properties command higher median prices than suburban and rural properties. This confirms that location type (urban vs. rural) is a meaningful price driver beyond just the state.

Insight 5: Florida, Texas, and California Have the Most Listings¶

These three states dominate the dataset in volume, reflecting their large and active real estate markets. California and Hawaii lead in median price, while states like West Virginia and Mississippi are the most affordable.

Conclusion¶

The analysis confirms the hypothesis: property location has a stronger impact on home prices than the number of bedrooms. Geographic factors — state, city, and area type — explain a larger portion of price variation than property-level characteristics. For buyers, sellers, and investors, location should be the primary consideration when evaluating property values.
